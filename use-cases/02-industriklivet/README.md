# **Systemic Background: ETS, CRCF and the Role of ACE in a Fossil‑Constrained Europe**

---

## **1. Continued Fossil Use Requires Corresponding CDR — and ACE Provides the Missing Link**

Under the **EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS)**, industrial emitters must surrender EU Allowances (EUAs) for every tonne of **positive emissions**.  
Even with rapid decarbonisation, many hard‑to‑abate sectors will continue to rely on fossil energy for decades.

In parallel, the **Carbon Removal Certification Framework (CRCF)** defines how **negative emissions** must be measured, verified and certified to count toward EU climate objectives.

However:

- ETS governs **emissions**  
- CRCF governs **removals**  
- **no EU mechanism connects the two**

This creates a structural gap:

> **ETS‑regulated emitters cannot directly use CRCF‑certified removals in a transparent, auditable and interoperable way.**

As long as positive emissions persist under ETS, an equivalent quantity of certified negative emissions is required to reach net‑zero — but the EU lacks the operational infrastructure to connect them.

**ACE provides that missing connection.**  
ACE is the neutral infrastructure layer linking **ETS‑positive emissions** with **CRCF‑certified negative emissions**, enabling issuance, transfer and retirement of high‑integrity CDR certificates (ACE‑CERT) in a unified, audit‑ready system.

---

## **2. Biomass‑Based CDR: Cost‑Effective Today, Physically Limited**

Within CRCF, biomass‑based CDR pathways (BECCS, biochar, long‑lived biogenic materials, regenerative agriculture) are:

- technologically mature  
- cost‑effective in the near term  
- deployable today  

But they are constrained by **biophysical limits**:

> Even if the entire global land surface were converted to high‑productivity biomass, photosynthesis could only compensate for ~20% of current fossil emissions.

This limit is driven by:

- solar energy availability  
- photosynthetic efficiency  
- land and water constraints  
- competition with food, forestry and ecosystems  

**Biomass‑CDR is essential — but inherently insufficient to balance ETS‑scale emissions.**

---

## **3. Ocean‑Based CDR: High Potential, Not Yet Certifiable**

The oceans hold the world’s largest natural carbon sink, but ocean‑based CDR currently lacks:

- CRCF‑compliant MRV  
- permanence models  
- certification frameworks  
- ecological risk assessments  

**Ocean CDR is a future CRCF category — not a present one.**

---

## **4. DACCS: The Only Fully Scalable Non‑Biomass Solution**

Direct Air Carbon Capture and Storage (DACCS) is the only CDR pathway that:

- does not require land  
- does not require biomass  
- does not compete with food or forestry  
- can scale without biophysical limits  
- provides permanent geological storage  

DACCS is the only technology capable of matching **ETS‑scale residual emissions** in the long term.

But DACCS is constrained by:

- high capital cost  
- high operating cost  
- lack of stable CO₂ price signals  
- absence of bankable, standardized certification  

CRCF provides the regulatory foundation —  
**ACE provides the operational infrastructure.**

---

## **6. The Price Ladder: Biomass → ACE‑CERT → DACCS**

The transition to large‑scale CDR follows a predictable sequence:

1. Biomass‑CDR is the lowest‑cost CRCF‑compliant entry point.  
2. ACE certifies these removals, enabling transparent ACE‑CERT issuance.  
3. Industrial ETS emitters purchase ACE‑CERT to compensate residual emissions.  
4. Demand increases → CO₂e prices rise and stabilize.  
5. Higher, predictable prices make **DACCS investable**.  
6. DACCS scales to become the long‑term backbone of permanent removals.  
7. Biomass‑CDR remains a valuable but limited complement.

This mirrors the historical ETS trajectory:  
**low‑cost technologies establish the market; high‑capex technologies scale once price signals mature.**

## **8. Use Case 02 — Industrial Baseline and Regenerative CDR Pairing**

This use case demonstrates how ACE quantifies:

- a **fossil industrial baseline** (positive emissions)  
- a **regenerative agricultural scenario** (negative emissions per hectare)  

and how these can be **paired quantitatively** to reach a net‑zero outcome at certificate level.

### **Industrial Baseline — ACE‑CERT (Industry)**

- **Cost:** 1 000 000 SEK  
- **Land use:** `other`  
- **Fossil CO₂e:** ≈ +35.9 t  
- **Net CO₂e:** ≈ +35.9 t  

A pure fossil baseline.

### **Regenerative Agriculture — ACE‑CERT (1 ha)**

- **Net CO₂e:** ≈ –0.716 t/ha  
- **All pools and N‑flows accounted for**  

A high‑integrity CRCF‑aligned biological CDR unit.

### **Pairing Logic**

\[
\frac{35.9}{0.716} \approx 50 \text{ ha}
\]

Thus:

- **50 ha regenerative agriculture ≈ –35.8 t CO₂e**  
- **Industrial process ≈ +35.9 t CO₂e**  

→ **Net ≈ 0 t CO₂e**

ACE makes this pairing **explicit, reproducible and audit‑ready**.

# **Systemic Insight**

This use case demonstrates a fundamental property of ACE:

> **All biological CDR is expressed in the same denominator — CO₂e per hectare — making biological CDR fungible, interoperable and mathematically compatible across domains.**

As a result:

- any industrial ACE‑CERT (positive emissions)  
- can be matched with any portfolio of biological ACE‑CERT (negative emissions)  

regardless of whether the land is cropland, forest or perennial/vall.

This is not a domain‑specific mechanism.  
It is a **structural feature of ACE** — a unified carbon accounting model enabling deterministic matching and cryptographic verification.

Because ACE exposes all flows in CO₂e per hectare, the **true scale requirement** becomes visible:

- modest fossil processes require **tens of hectares**  
- medium‑scale systems require **hundreds**  
- national systems require **millions**

ACE does not hide this gap — it **reveals it**.

And by doing so, ACE sets the stage for the next step:

> **Use Case 03: When biological CDR is insufficient at system scale, the residual must be covered by DACCS‑based ACE‑CERT — not by further over‑booking of biomass.**
