# **Use Case 03 — Biomass Limits (DACCS)**

The previous use case established a core ACE principle:  
**all biological CDR is expressed in the same denominator — CO₂e per hectare — making regenerative agriculture, forest and perennial systems fungible and quantitatively matchable with industrial ACE‑CERT.**

This reveals the true scale requirement with mathematical clarity.  
And once the scale is visible, the **system‑level consequence** becomes unavoidable.

---

## **1. Biological CDR Is Finite and Biomass‑Bound**

All biological CDR — regardless of domain — is ultimately constrained by:

- available land  
- photosynthetic efficiency  
- biomass turnover  
- ecological limits  
- competition with food, forestry and biodiversity  

Even when expressed in a unified ACE metric, the **total biological CDR potential is capped** by the amount of biomass the biosphere can produce and sustain.

ACE does not estimate this limit — it **exposes** it.

---

## **2. Industrial Emissions Exceed Biological Capacity**

When industrial ACE‑CERT are matched against biological ACE‑CERT portfolios, the arithmetic becomes explicit:

- modest fossil processes require **tens of hectares**  
- medium‑scale industrial systems require **hundreds**  
- national‑scale residual emissions require **millions**  

ACE makes this mismatch visible in a way that cannot be negotiated away or hidden behind accounting conventions.

The conclusion is structural:

> **Biological CDR is necessary, but not sufficient, to balance industrial emissions at system scale.**

---

## **3. Sweden as a System‑Scale Example**

ACE makes it possible to quantify the national biological CDR ceiling with precision.

### **Sweden’s biological domains**

| Domain | Area (ha) |
|--------|-----------|
| Forest (productive) | **28.0 M ha** |
| Cropland | **2.7 M ha** |
| Perennial/vall | **1.1 M ha** |

Total biological area: 31.8 million ha

#### Waldenlind Constant (W)

The **Waldenlind Constant** is a biological constant defined by ACE.  
It captures a simple but powerful observation:

> **Well‑managed living systems across ecological domains tend to approach the same characteristic net carbon uptake.**

Formally:

\[
W = 0.7 \ \text{t C ha^{-1} yr^{-1}}
\]

Converted to CO₂‑equivalents:

\[
W \times 3.67 \approx 2.6 \ \text{t CO₂e ha^{-1} yr^{-1}}
\]

#### What makes W a *real* constant?

W behaves like other scientific constants that describe large‑scale natural patterns —  
not unlike **H₀** (Hubble constant) in cosmology or **Q₁₀** in biology.

It qualifies as a real constant because:

- it emerges **independently** in forests, regenerative cropland, grasslands, and agroforestry  
- it reflects **fundamental ecological constraints** (photosynthesis, respiration, soil carbon stabilization)  
- it is **stable as an order of magnitude** across climates and management systems  
- it is **empirically measurable** and **scientifically falsifiable**

In other words:  
**ACE defines the constant, but nature supplies the evidence.**

#### Not a ceiling — but near to the biological upper range

W is **not** a hard maximum.  
Biological systems can exceed it temporarily, especially during early regeneration phases.

But across long time spans, well‑managed systems tend to operate near:

\[
0.5 \text{–} 1.0 \ \text{t C ha^{-1} yr^{-1}}
\]

with **0.7** as the most representative, cross‑domain value.

This makes W a **near‑upper‑range biological invariant** —  
a realistic target that healthy ecosystems can approach.

#### ACE‑Value per Hectare

ACE uses the Waldenlind Constant as the baseline biological flux:

\[
\text{ACE‑value per hectare} = - W \times 3.67
\]

\[
\text{ACE‑value per hectare} \approx -2.6 \ \text{t CO₂e ha^{-1} yr^{-1}}
\]

The negative sign indicates **net removal of CO₂ from the atmosphere**.

This gives ACE a **single, domain‑agnostic KPI** for evaluating biological performance across:

- forests  
- regenerative agriculture  
- perennial grasslands  
- mixed perennial systems  

### **Maximum biological CDR**

\[
31.8 \text{ M ha} \times 0.7 \approx 22 \text{ Mt CO₂e/yr}
\]

### **Sweden’s emissions**

- National emissions: **≈45 Mt CO₂e/yr**  
- Biological CDR max: **≈22 Mt CO₂e/yr**  
- **Residual:**  
  \[
  45 - 22 = 23 \text{ Mt CO₂e/yr}
  \]

This is an **ontological mismatch**, not a political one.

---

## **4. BECCS and Biochar Do Not Solve the Constraint**

BECCS and biochar are technologically distinct, but remain **biomass‑dependent**.  
They cannot exceed the physical limit imposed by:

- land availability  
- biomass growth rates  
- ecological sustainability  

ACE treats them correctly:

> **as transformations of the same finite biological carbon pool — not as new sources of CDR.**

Thus:

> **No biomass‑based pathway can scale beyond the biosphere’s inherent limits.**

---

## **5. The Residual Must Be Covered by Non‑Biomass CDR**

Once ACE reveals the finite nature of biological CDR, the system‑level consequence follows directly:

- residual emissions that exceed biological capacity  
- **must** be balanced by CDR that does not rely on biomass  
- and therefore does not compete with land, food or ecosystems  

This leaves only one category:

### **DACCS — the only scalable, non‑biomass CDR pathway**

DACCS is not introduced as a preference or policy choice.  
It emerges as a **logical necessity** once ACE exposes the biomass constraint.

---

## **6. Sweden’s Structural Advantage: Energy for DACCS**

DACCS requires:

- stable electricity  
- low marginal cost  
- low carbon intensity  
- high operating hours  

Sweden has:

- **hydropower** (stable, dispatchable, fossil‑free)  
- **nuclear baseload**  
- **cold air** (lower DACCS energy demand)  
- **geological storage access** (North Sea)  

This makes Sweden one of Europe’s **most favourable DACCS locations**.

ACE reveals this by:

- quantifying the biological ceiling  
- quantifying the national residual  
- quantifying the DACCS requirement  
- exposing Sweden’s unique energy advantage  

---

## **7. ACE as a System Diagnostic**

By expressing all biological CDR in CO₂e per hectare and matching it against industrial ACE‑CERT, ACE becomes:

- a **diagnostic tool** for national and sectoral net‑zero pathways  
- a **quantitative boundary detector** for biomass‑based CDR  
- a **decision engine** for when DACCS becomes unavoidable  

ACE does not prescribe solutions.  
It reveals the **mathematical structure** of the problem.

And that structure leads to a single systemic conclusion:

> **When biological CDR is insufficient, the residual must be covered by DACCS‑based ACE‑CERT — not by further over‑booking of biomass.**
